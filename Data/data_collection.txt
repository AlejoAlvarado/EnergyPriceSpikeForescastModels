What the CSD Historical Generation Data contains:
It's the same data that powers AESO's real-time Current Supply & Demand page, stored as CSV files organized by month or year. Each row is one time interval (hourly and 5-minute versions available) and each column is an individual generating unit. The key difference from your existing CSVs is that each unit comes with a fuel type label — so you can immediately filter for WIND and SOLAR rows and sum them to get province-wide totals per hour.
The files cover approximately 2018 onward and are updated monthly, so you'd have coverage through early 2026 if needed.

My honest take on whether it's worth it:
The 2020–2025 window is actually more interesting from a modeling perspective because solar became significant in Alberta after 2022 — the Travers Solar project (465 MW) came online in 2023 and was Canada's largest at the time. So your model would be trained on a period where both wind AND solar meaningfully affect prices, which makes the problem richer.
